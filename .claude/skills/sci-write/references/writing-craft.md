# Writing craft — the mechanics of precise scientific prose

Distilled July 2026 from: Gopen & Swan, "The Science of Scientific Writing" (Am. Scientist 1990,
via crowl.org/Lawrence/writing/GopenSwan90.html); Mensh & Kording, "Ten simple rules for
structuring papers" (PLOS Comput Biol 2017, doi:10.1371/journal.pcbi.1005619); Schimel,
*Writing Science* (2012); Bourne & Chalupa, "Ten simple rules for getting grants" (PLOS Comput
Biol 2006); Swales' CARS model; NIH/NIAID aims-page guidance.

## A. Sentence mechanics (Gopen & Swan — reader-expectation theory)

Meaning is not what the writer intends but what the reader infers from *where* information sits.
Readers assign roles by position:

1. **Topic position (sentence start) = old information.** Open each sentence with what links
   back to the previous one; this is what "flow of thought" mechanically is.
2. **Stress position (sentence end) = new, emphatic information.** Readers emphasize whatever
   arrives at syntactic closure. Put the point of the sentence last. Colons/semicolons create
   secondary stress positions in long sentences.
3. **Subject and verb stay adjacent.** Material wedged between them is read as parenthetical
   and burns attention.
4. **The action lives in the verb.** Nominalizations ("achieved a reduction in") hide the event;
   write "reduced". Schimel's fuzzy-verb blacklist: occur, affect, facilitate, perform, conduct,
   "had an influence on".
5. **Whose story is the sentence telling?** Choose the grammatical subject accordingly
   ("Bees disperse pollen" vs "Pollen is dispersed by bees" are different stories). Passive
   voice is legitimate only as this deliberate choice.
6. **Chain sentences stress→topic**: the new information at the end of one sentence becomes the
   old information opening the next. Topic→topic chaining produces lists, not arguments —
   this is the difference between flowing and choppy prose.
7. **One unit, one job**: each sentence one point, each paragraph one point (stated in a
   topic sentence ~70% of the time; point-last only when synthesizing), each section one function.

## B. Document architecture

- **Context → Content → Conclusion at every scale** (Mensh & Kording): the paper, each section,
  each paragraph orients first, delivers second, lands the takeaway last. Structure is fractal.
- **One central contribution per paper**, stated in the title. If you can't sketch the argument
  structure to a colleague in two minutes, readers won't reconstruct it either.
- **No zig-zag**: touch the central thread often, every side-topic exactly once, grouped.
  Use identical wording for identical concepts — a synonym signals a different concept.
- **Allocate polish by readership**: title > abstract > figures > intro/discussion > methods.
  Figure titles state conclusions; legends state method. Abstract = complete story in miniature:
  context → gap → why it matters → method → result → interpretation.
- **Match opening and resolution width** (Schimel's hourglass): open wider than you resolve and
  you overpromised; resolve wider than you opened and you undersold.
- **The "so what" test**: every result must connect back to the opening problem. "More research
  is needed" is a banned resolution.

## C. The introduction funnel (papers) — the user's required flow, formalized

Swales' CARS model, converging with Mensh & Kording Rule 6 and Schimel's Opening→Challenge:

1. **Move 1 — Establish the territory (why this problem matters).** Open on the problem's real
   stakes (deaths, cost, physical unknown, societal need) with a number and citation, not on
   your subfield's internal conversation and not on a schema every reader already holds.
2. **Move 2 — Establish the niche (review → gap).** Review prior work in progressively narrowing
   paragraphs, each ending on what remains unknown. The gap must emerge *from* the review —
   the reader should feel the objective coming before it is stated ("however, little is known
   about X"; "these approaches assume Y, which fails when…"). Never bolt on "but nobody has
   done exactly this" after a neutral literature summary; critique and synthesize so the gap
   is the review's inevitable conclusion.
3. **Move 3 — Occupy the niche.** State the objective/question, announce the approach and (in
   papers) the principal findings. Questions and hypotheses beat vague objectives: "focusing on
   objectives instead of questions is weak science and weak storytelling" (Schimel).

## D. Proposal architecture — different arc from papers

Reviewers are **impatient** readers; journal readers are patient. Papers can run
Opening–Challenge–Action–Resolution; proposals must front-load (Schimel's LD/LDR):
state the important problem and your answer to it early, then develop.

- **The first page decides the score.** Unassigned panelists read only the aims/summary;
  assigned reviewers form their view in the first minutes, then hunt for confirmation.
- **Four-paragraph opening anatomy** (NIH aims-page pattern, transfers to any scheme):
  (1) field-wide problem → the specific gap, in plain language; (2) central hypothesis/idea in
  one sentence + what you will do + why you/your host can; (3) 2–4 numbered objectives, each
  with approach and expected outcome, related but *independent* (one failing must not kill the
  rest); (4) impact — what changes in science and society if it works, phrased so a reviewer
  can repeat it in one sentence to the panel. "A clear aims page arms your advocate in the room."
- **Novel but not speculative**; over-proposing reads as overambitious and is rated *down*.
  Think small, propose less, with clear endpoints a reviewer can assess.
- **Answer the call text specifically** — proposals are scored against the funder's printed
  criteria, not against abstract quality. Follow the template; non-compliance dies before review.
- **No results yet, so credibility comes from**: preliminary work/publications mapped onto the
  objectives, method specificity (named data, named instruments, named algorithms with fallbacks),
  a feasible timeline, and honest risks with mitigations.
- **Impact section**: separate the science advance (what the field can do afterwards that it
  cannot do now) from the societal path (who uses it, via which channel — early warning centers,
  codes/standards, policy, open datasets), each with concrete, checkable claims.

## E. Process rules (grants and papers)

- Outline first (one sentence per paragraph); rewrite rather than patch — vague reader feedback
  means the story structure is broken, not the wording.
- Get external readers who resemble the real panel, including one outside the field to expose
  jargon; for MSCA use the free NCP proofreading (KoWi for German hosts).
- Reviewers are tired humans reading many proposals outside their subfield: clear structure,
  flagged key points, readable figures are scoring instruments, not decoration.
- Treat rejection as data: resubmissions that answer every criticism explicitly and factually
  routinely convert (the funded Shannon Chance MSCA was a revised rejection).
